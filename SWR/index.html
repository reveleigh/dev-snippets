Expanded Noun Phrases

An expanded noun phrase adds more detail to a noun by using modifiers such as adjectives, adverbs, prepositional phrases, and relative clauses.

The tall, old tree in the park that swayed gently in the breeze.

A sleek, silver sports car roared down the highway.

The ancient, crumbling castle perched atop the windswept hill.

Those vibrant, hand-painted ceramic bowls were displayed on the shelf.

The student with the bright red backpack sat in the front row.

Passive, Active, and Modal Verbs

Passive Verbs: The subject of the sentence receives the action.
Active Verbs: The subject of the sentence performs the action.
Modal Verbs: Auxiliary verbs that express possibility, necessity, permission, etc.

Passive: The letter was written by John.

Active: John wrote the letter.

Passive: The cake was eaten by the children.

Active: The children ate the cake.

Modal: I might be able to solve this puzzle.

Speech Marks (Inverted Commas): Direct and Reported Speech

Speech Marks (Inverted Commas): Punctuation marks (" ") used to indicate direct speech or quotations.
Reported Speech: Reports what was said without using quotation marks, often changing the tense and pronouns.

Direct Speech: She said, "Hello, how are you?"

Reported Speech: She asked how I was.

Direct Speech: He exclaimed, "I won the game!"

Reported Speech: He announced that he had won the game.

Embedded, Subordinated, and Main Clauses

Embedded Clause: A clause that is contained within another clause and adds extra information.
Subordinated Clause: A clause that cannot stand alone as a sentence and depends on a main clause.
Main Clause: A clause that can stand alone as a complete sentence and contains a subject and a verb.

Embedded: The student who studied diligently passed the exam.

Subordinated: Since the student studied diligently, they passed the exam.

Main: The student passed the exam after studying diligently.

Fronted Adverbials

An adverbial (a word, phrase, or clause that modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverbial) placed at the beginning of a sentence, before the subject and verb, to add emphasis or create a specific effect.

Manner: Quickly, she ran down the stairs.

Time: Yesterday afternoon, they finished the project.

Place: At the edge of the forest, a small cottage stood.

Frequency: Often, he would take long walks in the park.

Reason: Because of the heavy rain, the event was cancelled.

Conjunctions

Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS): Join independent clauses of equal importance. (For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So)
Subordinating Conjunctions: Join a dependent clause to an independent clause, showing a relationship between them.

Coordinating: I like tea and coffee.

Subordinating: We stayed inside because it was raining.

Coordinating: She can sing but she can't dance.

Subordinating: Although it was late, they continued working.

Coordinating: Do you want tea or coffee?

Figurative Language

Simile: Compares two unlike things using "like" or "as."
Metaphor: States that one thing is another, without using "like" or "as."

Simile: The snow was as white as a sheet, blanketing the entire field.

Metaphor: The classroom was a zoo.

Simile: He ran like a cheetah, a blur of motion across the track.

Metaphor: Her words were a knife, sharp and cutting through the silence.

Simile: The stars shone like diamonds, scattered across the velvet sky.

Sentence Fragments

An incomplete sentence that is missing a subject, a verb, or does not express a complete thought.

Running down the street. (Missing a subject)

The old, broken car. (Missing a verb)

When the bell rang. (Incomplete thought)

And then what? (Missing subject and verb)

To the store. (Missing subject and verb)
